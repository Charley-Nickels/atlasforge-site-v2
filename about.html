About

AtlasForge is a playground for stacks that don’t exist yet.

The project sits between tools and worlds. It treats workflows, routing, and sound the same way a game treats physics or weather — as systems that should feel consistent wherever you touch them.

AtlasStudio is the surface, Atlas-V is the language underneath, and worlds like Octopus in Action and S/K/B Audio Lab keep everything grounded in characters, cities, and sound.

Umbrella identity

Magenta energy core, orbit lines, and clean geometry tie everything together.

Origin

Built from spreadsheets, game ideas, and “what if…?” notes.

AtlasForge grew out of design logs, build trackers, and half-finished game concepts that all wanted the same thing: a shared way to talk about work, routes, risks, and versions.

Workstation mindset

The workstation exists first. Tools, worlds, and experiments plug into it instead of each having their own one-off UI.

Engine language

Navigator, Memory, Guard, and FlowSim give projects a shared vocabulary. Worlds can be wildly different and still feel like they belong in the same stack.

World-first design

Everything is tested against living worlds — like OIA’s harbor city — so flows stay connected to characters and places instead of abstract diagrams.

Brand family

One geometry language, five different jobs.

The logos share rings, lattices, and shards. The color and tone signal which part of the stack you’re dealing with.

Current focus

Make the fake stack feel real enough to design inside.

The immediate goal is to get AtlasStudio, Atlas-V, OIA, and S/K/B talking to each other in a way that feels like a working product — even while deeper systems evolve underneath.

AtlasStudio sessions

Atlas-V diagrams

OIA + S/K/B integration
